Your Dog Can't Tell You They're Dying. These 5 Signals Are How They Try.

8 min read · Updated May 2026 · Reviewed for accuracy

Cover image

Dogs are stoic by design. In the wild, showing weakness meant becoming a target. That evolutionary wiring is still running in your golden retriever, your terrier, your mutt who sleeps belly-up on the couch. When something is seriously wrong, they don't cry for help. They hide it. And by the time the signs are obvious enough that you Google them at midnight, the problem may have been building for weeks.

These five signals should end the Googling and start a phone call to your vet.

1. They stopped eating, and it's been more than 24 hours

A dog skipping one meal might have a mildly upset stomach. A dog refusing food for a full day — especially a dog who normally inhales their kibble — is telling you something is wrong. Loss of appetite is the single most common early indicator of serious illness in dogs, from kidney failure to cancer to intestinal blockage.

The exception: a single skipped meal after extreme heat, hard exercise, or excitement is usually nothing. A pattern of skipped meals across a full day or longer is not.

2. They're drinking way more water than usual

You're filling the bowl twice as often and you don't know why. Excessive thirst is a hallmark of diabetes, kidney disease, Cushing's syndrome, and liver problems. It's easy to miss because it happens gradually. If you're noticing it, it's probably been escalating for a while.

Try this: measure how much water you put in their bowl one morning. Measure how much is left 24 hours later. A rough guideline is one ounce of water per pound of body weight per day. A 50-pound dog drinking significantly more than 50 ounces consistently is worth a vet call.

3. They're hiding or withdrawing

Your social, velcro dog suddenly wants to be alone. They're sleeping in a different room. They don't greet you at the door. Dogs in pain or distress often isolate. This isn't them being "moody." This is a red flag with fur on it.

Behavioral change is one of the earliest and most reliable signs that something is wrong. You know your dog better than any vet does. Trust the gut feeling that says "they're not themselves."

4. They yelp or flinch when you touch a specific area

This one seems obvious, but owners explain it away constantly — "he just doesn't like being touched there." Healthy dogs don't yelp when you pet their abdomen. Healthy dogs don't snap when you touch near their hips. Localized pain means something localized is wrong.

If your normally cuddly dog has developed a "no-touch zone," that zone needs a professional opinion.

5. Their gums aren't pink

Lift your dog's lip right now. Healthy gums are bubblegum pink and moist. This is a two-second check that emergency vets wish every owner knew. Here's what colors can indicate:

  • White or pale. Anemia or internal bleeding. Emergency.
  • Blue or grey. Oxygen isn't circulating. Emergency.
  • Bright red. Overheating, toxicity, or severe infection. Urgent.
  • Sticky or dry. Dehydration. Could be mild, could be severe.
  • Yellow. Possible liver problem or red blood cell breakdown. Vet call.

The bottom line

None of these mean your dog is dying today. But every one of them means your dog needs a professional, not a search engine. The vet bill you're afraid of is always cheaper than the one you get by waiting.

Trust the gut feeling that says "they're not themselves." You know your dog better than anyone.
