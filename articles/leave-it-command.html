Forget 'Sit.' The One Command That Could Actually Save Your Dog's Life.

7 min read · Updated May 2026

Cover image
A trainer's closed fist held out in front of an attentive young puppy on a clean wood floor. Puppy is focused, ears slightly forward. Soft, even natural light from a side window.
See IMAGE_PROMPTS.md → article-leaveit

Every training guide starts with "sit." It's clean. It's easy. The dog does it, you feel like a genius, everyone's happy. But "sit" has never stopped a dog from eating a chicken bone off the sidewalk, lunging toward a moving car, or swallowing a sock that costs $3,000 to surgically remove.

"Leave it" has.

"Leave it" is the emergency brake of dog commands. It's the difference between your dog investigating a pile of chocolate at a kid's birthday party and your dog walking away from it. Between a close call with a rattlesnake and a catastrophe. Between a funny story and a funeral.

How to teach it, starting tonight

You need nothing but kibble and patience. Here's the entire foundation drill:

Hold a treat in your closed fist. Let your dog sniff, lick, paw at it — do nothing. The second they pull their nose away, even for a moment, even by accident, say "yes" and give them a different treat from your other hand. Not the one in your fist. A different one.

That's the entire concept

The thing you want is off-limits. The reward comes from turning away from it. Repeat this for five minutes. Within three sessions, most dogs will look away from your closed fist on purpose. That's the lightbulb moment.

The progression

From there, the difficulty ramps up gradually:

  1. Treat under your open hand on the floor. If they go for it, you cover it. Reward the moment they back off.
  2. Treat on the floor, uncovered, your hand nearby. Same rules.
  3. Increasingly interesting objects. A piece of cheese. A chicken treat. A sock. A shoe.
  4. Outside in a quiet environment. Then a noisier one.
  5. Real-world scenarios. A dropped french fry on a walk. A squirrel across the street.

The progression takes weeks, not months. Five minutes a day is more than enough.

Why this beats 'sit' on the priority list

"Sit" is a useful command for polite greetings and vet visits. Train it. But it's not what's going to keep your dog alive when they spot something dangerous before you do.

"Leave it" generalizes. Once a dog truly understands the concept — that the cue means "ignore the thing in front of you, the reward comes from looking away" — they can apply it to anything. A pill on the floor. A bone left by another dog at the park. A child running past with food. A car. A coyote.

The bottom line

"Sit" is a party trick. "Leave it" is a life skill. Train both. But if you only have ten minutes tonight, you know which one matters more.
