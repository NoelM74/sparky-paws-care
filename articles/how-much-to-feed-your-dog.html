You're Probably Feeding Your Dog Wrong. Here's How to Fix It Tonight.

6 min read · Updated May 2026

Cover image

Most dog owners pour kibble into a bowl until it "looks about right." No judgment — nearly everyone starts there. But that casual scoop could be shaving years off your dog's life, and the feeding guidelines on the back of the bag are partly to blame.

Those charts are designed to sell more food. They're calculated for highly active dogs and rounded up generously. An average 50-pound dog with a normal activity level needs roughly 700–900 calories a day. Most owners are feeding 1,200 or more without realizing it. The dog isn't "big-boned." The dog is overfed.

The number one health problem in domestic dogs isn't disease. It's obesity.

The fix takes sixty seconds

Look up your dog's ideal weight — not their current weight, their ideal weight for their breed and frame. Multiply that number by 30, then add 70. That's a rough daily calorie target in a formula veterinary nutritionists actually use.

Now flip your food bag over and check the calories per cup. Divide your target by that number. That's how many cups per day. Split it into two meals.

When the formula doesn't apply

If your dog is a puppy, a senior, pregnant, or a working breed, the math shifts — talk to your vet. The 30× + 70 formula targets adult dogs at maintenance. Highly active dogs (think Border Collies on a working farm, or hiking companions doing 10+ miles a weekend) may genuinely need more. Couch-dwelling seniors often need 10–20% less.

But for the average adult dog lying on your couch right now, this formula is a better starting point than anything printed on that bag.

The bottom line

The number one health problem in domestic dogs isn't disease. It's obesity. And the fix isn't expensive food or supplements. It's a measuring cup and five minutes of honesty.

Weigh your dog. Find their ideal weight (your vet can tell you in 30 seconds at your next visit, or look up breed standards online for a starting point). Do the math. Measure the food. Two meals a day, no free-feeding from a bottomless bowl. That's it.

Your dog will live longer. Their joints will thank you. Their vet bills will shrink. And the only thing you've spent is the cost of a $4 measuring cup.
