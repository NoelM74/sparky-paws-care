This Summer, the Sidewalk Could Burn Your Dog's Paws Off. Do the 5-Second Test Before Every Walk.

5 min read · Updated May 2026

At 77°F outside, asphalt surface temperature hits 125°F. At 87°F outside, it reaches 143°F. An egg fries at 131°F. Your dog's paw pads blister and suffer permanent tissue damage at 120°F.

Read that again.

The air temperature you feel on your face has almost nothing to do with what your dog feels underfoot. Concrete and asphalt absorb and radiate heat in ways that turn a pleasant afternoon into a veterinary emergency. Every summer, thousands of dogs are treated for paw burns that their owners never saw coming — because the weather app said 82 and sunny and that sounded fine.

The test that could save your dog a world of pain

Place the back of your hand flat against the pavement. Hold it there for five full seconds.

If you can't make it to five without pulling away, your dog can't walk on it. Period. Not even for a quick bathroom trip. Not even "just to the corner."

What to do when the ground fails the test

You have options. None of them require canceling your day:

  • Walk on grass. Grass surface temperature stays close to air temperature even on hot days.
  • Shift the schedule. Walk in the early morning before the sun has heated the pavement, or after sunset once it's cooled.
  • Get protective booties. Yes, dogs look ridiculous in them. No, they don't care once they're used to them.
  • Stay inside. A few days of indoor play and mental enrichment won't hurt your dog. A burned paw will.

The hidden danger: delayed pain

The worst part about paw burns is that dogs won't always show pain immediately. They'll limp hours later, or you'll notice raw, peeling pads the next morning and have no idea when it happened. By then, the damage is done — and treatment is painful, slow, and expensive.

Some dogs are particularly stoic and will keep walking on burning pavement to keep up with you. Their loyalty doesn't override basic physics. Their pads will still cook.

The rule for the rest of summer

Five seconds. Back of your hand. Every single time, all summer long. It takes less time to do the test than it takes to read this paragraph.

If you're not sure, you don't walk. Your dog has four feet of skin pressed directly against the ground. Yours has two protected by shoes. The asymmetry matters.
